UAV Motor Bench Tester

Automated thrust, power, and thermal test rig for brushless UAV power systems — built around an ESP32, load cell and browser-based dashboard.

ESP32 Web dashboard Load cell thrust rig Automated test sequences

Overview

This custom UAV motor test rig is a complete bench platform for characterising motors, propellers and ESCs. An ESP32 controls the throttle signal, reads thrust from a load cell via an HX711 amplifier, and serves a browser-based dashboard that streams real-time thrust, current and voltage data.

The dashboard supports both manual throttle control and a fully automated Dynamic Test Sequence system, where you can stack ramp and hold steps to create repeatable test profiles — ideal for comparing different props, motors or cooling configurations. Built-in arming logic, soft-start ramps and rate limits keep the tests smooth and reduce stress on the ESC.

All data can be logged and exported to CSV for deeper analysis in Excel or your preferred tools. A dummy version of the interface is available here: UAV motor dashboard mock.

The entire rig — from CAD and 3D-printed parts through to electronics, firmware and web UI — was designed and built in-house. By leaning on AI-assisted coding and rapid iteration, I went from concept to a working, automated test platform in roughly two days of focused work.

Electronics & hardware

  • Controller: ESP32 development board (WebSocket server + PWM throttle output).
  • Thrust measurement: 0–10 kg load cell with HX711 amplifier.
  • Powertrain: Brushless motor under test, paired with a suitable UAV ESC.
  • Power source: LiPo battery dedicated to the ESC and motor.
  • Instrumentation: Watt meter / power analyzer for current and voltage readings.
  • Thermals: Handheld infrared thermometer for surface temperature checks.
  • Mechanical: Rigid thrust stand, motor mount and fixtures (machined / 3D printed).
  • Host device: Any phone, tablet or laptop with a modern browser to access the dashboard.

How it was used

Tests were configured directly on the dashboard as a sequence of ramp and hold steps. A typical Dynamic Test Sequence for a propulsion setup looked like this:

  • Ramp from 0 % to 20 % throttle over 5 seconds, hold for 5 seconds.
  • Ramp to 60 % over 5 seconds, hold for 60 seconds.
  • Ramp to 100 % over 5 seconds, hold for 20 seconds.
  • Ramp back down to 60 % over 5 seconds, hold for 30 seconds.
  • Ramp smoothly down to 0 % and disarm.

During each automated run the rig continuously logged thrust and telemetry from the ESP32. In parallel, current, voltage and temperature readings from the power analyzer and infrared thermometer were captured and entered into the dashboard at key points.

After a test, the full dataset was exported as a CSV file and analysed in Excel. This made it easy to compare different motors, propellers and cooling setups on equal terms, and to spot where efficiency dropped off or thermal limits were being approached.
